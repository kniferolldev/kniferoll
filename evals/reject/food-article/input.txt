Why Your Spiral-Cut Ham Always Dries Out

There's a reason spiral-cut ham is a holiday staple: the slices come pre-cut, making serving a crowd effortless. But that convenience comes with a cost. Those thin, pre-sliced layers have dramatically more exposed surface area than a whole uncut ham, which means they shed moisture far more quickly in the oven — often going from juicy to desiccated before the center has even warmed through.

The problem is compounded by the nature of cured ham itself. A fully cooked cured ham only needs to reach eating temperature (around 140°F internal). Yet most package instructions call for an hour or more in a 325–350°F oven, plenty of time for the outer layers to overcook while the thickest part of the roast inches toward temperature.

There's also a geometry issue. The spiral cut creates a sort of accordion structure: as the ham contracts from heat, the slices press together, squeezing juice outward and away. The liquid that drips into the pan is flavor and moisture that used to be in the meat.

All of this means that the standard approach — unwrapped ham, hot oven, long cook — is exactly backwards for what you actually want. Lower heat limits moisture loss through evaporation. Keeping the ham tightly wrapped in foil for most of its oven time creates a moist environment that slows drying. Pulling it at the lowest safe temperature (not the highest acceptable one) makes an enormous difference. And applying a glaze only at the very end, after removing the foil, lets you get a lacquered crust without the long unprotected exposure that dries things out.

None of this is complicated. It's just the opposite of what the packaging tells you to do.
